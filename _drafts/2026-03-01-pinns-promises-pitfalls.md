PINNs have generated enormous excitement in the scientific computing community over the past few years. They offer an elegant idea: encode the governing PDE directly into the loss function and let automatic differentiation do the rest. But the reality of training them on non-trivial problems — stiff equations, complex geometries, multi-scale physics — is considerably harder than the original papers suggest. In this post I share some honest observations from trying to apply PINNs to jet impingement heat transfer problems.
